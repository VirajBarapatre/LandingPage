1. What Is Tennis, in Simple Words?

Tennis is a game where two sides hit a ball back and forth over a net. If you hit the ball so that it lands inside the court and the other side cannot return it properly, you win the point.

1.1 Who plays and where?

You play tennis on a rectangular court with a net in the middle. The court can be outside or indoors. There are two main types of matches:

  • Singles: 1 vs 1 (most common in TV matches).
  • Doubles: 2 vs 2 (two players on each side).
1.2 What happens in one point?

Every rally (point) roughly follows this order:

  1. The right player serves (starts the point by hitting the ball into the opposite service box).
  2. The other player tries to return the serve back over the net.
  3. Both players hit the ball back and forth (this is called a rally).
  4. The point ends when the ball:
    • Hits the net and does not go over, or
    • Lands outside the court lines, or
    • Bounces twice before a player hits it.
1.3 How does the scoring work? (With example)

Tennis does not use 1, 2, 3, 4 for points inside a game. It uses: 0 (Love) → 15 → 30 → 40 → Game.

Example of one game between Player A and Player B:

  • Both start at 0–0 (Love–Love).
  • Player A wins the first point → score becomes 15–0.
  • Player B wins the next point → score becomes 15–15.
  • Player A wins again → 30–15.
  • Player A wins again → 40–15.
  • Player A wins one more point → Game to A.

If the score reaches 40–40, it is called deuce. Then a player must win two points in a row: first “advantage”, then “game”.

1.4 Basic match structure
From point to match
  • Point – one rally, winner gets 15/30/40/Game.
  • Game – first to reach “Game” with at least two points more than the opponent.
  • Set – usually first to 6 games, must lead by 2 games (e.g., 6–4). Often a tie-break is played at 6–6.
  • Match – usually best of 3 sets, or best of 5 sets in some men’s tournaments.
Very short summary

You win points to win a game, win games to win a set, and win sets to win the match.

2. How Does a Match Actually Happen?

If you sit down to watch a tennis match having never seen one before, this is roughly what you will see, step by step.

2.1 Before the match
Warm-up & coin toss
  • Players enter the court with their bags and rackets.
  • They meet at the net with the umpire (referee).
  • A coin toss decides who will serve first and which side each player starts on.
  • Players hit a few balls to warm up (usually 5–10 minutes).
2.2 During the match
Games and sets in action
  • The server stands behind the baseline, to the right side first.
  • They serve diagonally into the opponent’s service box.
  • After each point, the server serves again (two tries each time).
  • After each game, serving changes to the other player.
  • Players change ends of the court after every odd number of games (e.g., after game 1, 3, 5…).
2.3 How a simple set might look
Example: Player A vs Player B

Imagine Player A and Player B are playing a set. The game score could go like this:

  • Game 1: A wins → set score 1–0
  • Game 2: B wins → set score 1–1
  • Game 3: A wins → set score 2–1
  • … they continue until one reaches 6 games.
  • If the score is 6–4, the set is over (A wins 6–4).
  • If the score becomes 6–6, they usually play a tie-break to decide that set.
2.4 What is a tie-break?
Quick version

A tie-break is like a mini game used when the set is 6–6. Instead of normal 15/30/40 scoring, players count: 1, 2, 3…. The first to reach 7 points, with at least 2 points more than the opponent (for example 7–5, 8–6), wins the tie-break and the set 7–6.

You don’t need to know every detail to enjoy tennis. Just remember: a tie-break is a short, intense race for points to decide a close set.

3. Court, Lines & Equipment

If you have never seen a tennis court, the lines can look confusing. Here is what they mean and what you need to play.

3.1 Basic court layout (in words)
  • The court is a rectangle divided by a net in the middle.
  • Long lines at the far ends are called baselines.
  • Long lines on the sides are called sidelines (different for singles and doubles).
  • Shorter lines near the net and middle make two boxes on each side – these are the service boxes.
  • The ball must land in the correct service box on a serve.
3.2 Court sizes (you don’t need to memorize)

Length: 78 ft (23.77 m)

Singles width: 27 ft (8.23 m)

Doubles width: 36 ft (10.97 m)

Net height: 3 ft (0.914 m) in the middle

3.3 What you need to play
Equipment checklist
  • Racket: Choose a beginner racket, not too heavy.
  • Ball: Normal yellow tennis balls. Children often start with softer, slower balls.
  • Shoes: Sports shoes with non-marking soles. Some courts require this.
  • Clothes: Comfortable T-shirt, shorts or track pants, and maybe a cap.
  • Optional: Wristbands, overgrip (extra grip tape on handle), and a simple sports bag.

You do not need expensive gear to start. A basic racket, a few balls, and any clean sports shoes are enough at the beginning.

4. What Skills Do Tennis Players Use?

You do not need all of these skills to start, but this is what good players gradually develop over time.

Serve – starting the point

The serve is the shot that begins every point. Players toss the ball in the air and hit it into the opposite service box.

  • Learn a simple, repeatable toss.
  • Bend your knees and use your whole body, not just your arm.
  • At beginner level, aim for consistency, not power.
Groundstrokes – rallying

These are your main shots during a rally: forehand and backhand. They are usually hit after one bounce.

  • Turn your shoulders and prepare the racket early.
  • Watch the ball closely until it meets the strings.
  • Try to send the ball higher over the net and deeper into the court for safety.
Net play & touch

Volleys (hit before the bounce), overheads, and drop shots help finish points and surprise opponents.

  • Keep swings short at the net.
  • Hold the racket in front of you for quick reactions.
  • Use gentle hands for short, soft shots close to the net.
Footwork & fitness

Tennis is not just about the arm; it is a full-body sport. Split steps, quick first moves, and good recovery steps help you reach the ball on time and in balance.

Mental side

Staying calm after mistakes, focusing on one point at a time, and not giving up when you are behind are huge parts of the game. Even professionals struggle with this.

Simple strategy

Examples: play more shots to the opponent’s weaker side, bring them forward with a short ball, or push them back with a deeper shot. Strategy grows naturally as you play more.

6. The Simple Science Behind Tennis

You don’t need to be good at physics to enjoy tennis, but basic ideas explain why some shots dip down, fly long, or bounce high.

6.1 Ball flight: up, forward, down

When you hit a ball, it goes up and forward, then gravity pulls it back down. Players choose the height and speed so the ball lands inside the court and not into the net.

6.2 Topspin and slice

If you brush the ball from low to high, you create topspin. The ball rotates forward, which makes it dip down faster into the court. This lets players hit hard and still keep the ball in.

If you cut the ball from high to low, you create slice or backspin. The ball floats more and often bounces lower and slower. Slice is used to change rhythm and force awkward contact.

6.3 Strings and power

Racket strings act like a mini trampoline. Lower string tension can give more power and comfort, while higher tension can give more control. For beginners, comfort and control are usually more important than raw power.

7. Glossary – Common Tennis Words

Here are quick meanings of words you will often hear when watching or playing tennis.

Scoring & match terms
  • Love: zero points.
  • Deuce: 40–40, both players have three points in that game.
  • Advantage: one player wins a point after deuce and is one point away from winning the game.
  • Game: small unit of scoring, made of points.
  • Set: collection of games (usually first to 6, by 2).
  • Match: whole contest, best of 3 or 5 sets.
  • Tie-break: special game at 6–6 to decide a set.
Shot & play terms
  • Rally: sequence of shots between serve and end of the point.
  • Ace: serve that the opponent does not touch.
  • Volley: shot hit before the ball bounces, usually near the net.
  • Drop shot: very soft shot that lands just over the net.
  • Baseline: back line of the court at each end.
  • Break of serve: when the returning player wins the game (takes the other player’s serve).
  • Unforced error: a mistake made on a relatively easy shot.

8. Beginner Questions (FAQ)

If you are new to tennis, you might have these questions in mind. Here are short, clear answers.

Do I need to be very fit before I start?

No. You can start slowly with short sessions and simple rallies. Tennis itself helps you become fitter over time. Just listen to your body and take breaks.

Is tennis hard to learn?

Like any sport, it feels strange at first. But with a basic grip, a few lessons, and practice hitting gently over the net, most people can rally within a few weeks.

Can I play if I only know the very basic rules?

Absolutely. At first, just focus on getting the ball over the net and inside the court. You can learn full scoring gradually while you play.

Is tennis only for children or professionals?

No. Many adults start in their 20s, 30s, 40s or later. Clubs often have beginner groups for all ages. It is very much a lifelong sport.

Do I need a coach?

A coach helps you learn faster and avoid bad habits, but you can start by hitting with friends or against a wall. Even one or two basic lessons can be helpful.

Where should I watch tennis to understand more?

Watching highlights or full matches from Grand Slams on TV or online is a great way to see how points, games, and sets work together in real time.

9. Your First Steps to Actually Play

Here is a simple 3-step path from knowing nothing to playing your first friendly match.

Step 1
Try basic hits
  • Hold the racket with a comfortable, simple grip.
  • Stand a few metres from a friend and gently hit the ball back and forth.
  • Ignore power – aim only for getting the ball over the net.
Step 2
Join a local group
  • Find a tennis club, academy, or community courts near you.
  • Take beginner group sessions once or twice a week.
  • Ask the coach or players to explain scoring slowly while you play.
Step 3
Play a short match
  • Start with a “first to 4 games” match instead of a full set.
  • Serve slowly and safely so rallies can start.
  • Focus on enjoying each rally, not on the final result.

If you follow this page from top to bottom, you now know what the game is, how scoring works, what the courts and shots look like, and how to start playing in real life.
